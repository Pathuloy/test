My Story Eight years ago

I was in the darkest phase of my life. I was severely depressed and the act of leaving my bed was daunting. Being a freshman in high school, I attended a private college preparatory school (most of the time) and stayed home on days I needed to. I performed school tasks when I could (that is when there was a break from the confusion that overwhelms the depressed brain).

I attended private tutoring and was helped by both of my parents on school assignments. Support that was above and beyond what most parents are capable or willing to provide. It is without a doubt that my success in school that year is attributed to my parents’ determination and creativity.

I participated in softball (despite the bullying from my teammates. I kept putting one foot in front of the other but I didn’t know why. Other than the overwhelming pain that harming myself would bring my parents, I had no other reason to care for myself.

By care for myself I mean survive. I certainly was not engaging in the self care practices I live by today. In fact, the act of discussing my depression with my parents was cringe worthy. I thought that my depression was my burden and not my parents. After all they have given me and continue to provide me, why burden them with something as heavy as their child’s declining mental health? Who needs that tacked on to a long week at work? I learned the power of energy when being attracted to friends that were also severely depressed as if the dark cloud above me needed some storms. Being trapped in a whirlwind of hopelessness, my friends and I did our best to support each other when we ourselves could barely stand up. Doing my best to prevent catastrophic decisions by my friends such as suicide, I reached out the school counselor, began seeing a therapist outside of school, and recommended my therapist to a friend. A turning point of my path towards recovery occurred after my mom was discussing the upcoming school year as the summer came to a close. At some point in the conversation I burst into tears and begged to not go back to that school. My mom, embracing me in her arms, supported my decision and began brainstorming other options for my education. We decided I would attend a public performing arts high school in a different school district in hopes that dancing coupled with less scholastic demands would improve my mood. I began my first day of sophomore year in the emergency room as I recovered from repeated panic attacks and regurgitation of bile. Released just two hours before orientation, I took my ID photo with bloodshot eyes and a lot of teal eyeliner on. Things started getting better. There was a new environment and tasks I had to complete that kept me focused. I signed up for water polo (after I was not allowed on the college prep team at my former high school). I decided to do water polo because I was told I couldn’t. This started my drive for proving people wrong who had doubted me. I’ve learned now that you must prove your own doubts wrong as well and that just because you can do something doesn’t me you need to do it. You must choose which doubts are worth proving wrong and which people are worth putting their place. I look back and do not regret choosing to play water polo. It was the hardest I’ve ever worked physically and I just what I needed to slap myself into the present moment (even if for just a 24 minute game). Being competitive, I was hard on myself when I didn’t get to play because I wasn’t fast enough. I refused to use the excuse that I was half the physical size as my opponents. I refused to use my exercised-induced asthma as an excuse and I sure as heck refused to use my depression as an excuse. I worked hard every practice. I was the last one to leave the pool because I was cleaning up and making sure nobody left anything. I was often partnered with the second to slowest and smallest player. Her and I got along well. I wanted so hard to be her close friend. I wanted it too hard. I tirelessly to be everyone’s best friend by listening to them complain about school and life. I remained a shoulder to complain on and not a friend to depend on. I tried to prove that I was fun and dependable but I was not popular (far from it). I ate lunch in my coach’s classroom (she was also a science teacher). I cried in the bathrooms alone. I stayed to talk to my teachers because I wanted someone to talk to you. I decided that if I couldn’t have friends, I could at least have a high grade point average. I didn’t notice why I was able to focus on school tasks, I was just suddenly able to. I could manage group projects and coach my peers. I could take college level courses and engage in a two year research course. I would at least be acknowledged and recognized for my academic achievements. I realize now that I had a purpose greater than my darkest moments. I realize now the importance of stepping back and identifying what you are feeling. I also am able to do this now. The therapist back in my freshman year, tried to get me to identify my feelings and what I felt was emptiness. I thought the idea of identifying my mood is stupid and useless. It is okay if that’s how you feel. There is room to grow with that perspective. Now when I feel an unpleasant feeling, I identify the feeling, accept it, and keep moving forward. Eventually it goes away and it is not a waiting gain worth your time. Depression is like a bird that will come and sit on your shoulder. The duration of the visit varies, the visit comes often without notice, and is never welcomed, but the visit is happening. Accept that. Denial does nothing but temporarily repress depression that will bring back a whole family of depressed birds this time. Anyways, high school did not fly by for me (like others like to say it did for them). High school was like watching paint dry while buckets of wet paint were chucked at the wall. Eventually it was over. Thank freaking goodness. While I did grow in high school, well not that much. I am still 4’ 10” or 1.47m. But, seriously, I grew emotionally or rather I began to grow. As I worked as a cashier at a local car wash during the summer, I started experiencing panic attacks. I began seeing a psychiatrist who recommended I tackle my diet, exercise, and sleep before medicated treatment. For which, I responded in my head, how often do you feel like running, cooking, and sleeping during a panic attack? I was put on anti-anxiety medication. My psychiatrist really sold me on the medication saying something along the lines of, “This will not eliminate the panic attacks. This will just make it a little bit easier to get through the day”. I thought, “Really? I want a pill that will make me feel better now. Tonight at the latest.” She was right. She set an accurate expectation. I was able to get through the day but wasnt as vibrant as I used to be. Anti-anxiety medication works to regulate your serotonin levels which affect your sleep, ambition, and sense of accomplishment. I was not feeling so low but I was also not ever feeling high on life. Even during my darkest days, there were moments of relief that were pure joy. For instance, one of my fondest memories is being relieved of the excruciating pain I was in following the bile regurgitation. I felt so good lying there as I was finally not vomiting, nauseous, and dehydration. I was convinced I must look as good as I feel so I asked my sleep deprived father to take a photo of pale, smiling face. I posed like I was riding a horse. Let’s just say, I’ve had better photos taken since. Along, posting this photo as my Linkedin photo would be a conversation starter to say the least. Eventually, I decided to start exercising with Youtube videos, eating healthier, and sleeping more hours. I started feeling so much better, I called my physciatrist and ditched class to share my newfound health. I even apply for a job as a car sales associate and ended up meeting my now boyfriend of three years, Manuel. After discussing my experience with the anxiety medication with my new health conscious boyfriend, I decided to wean myself off the drug (per doctor’s guidance). My psychiatrist was more than supportive and explained that the medication was meant to help me get on my feet and not a lifelong prescription. Well I was on my feet (a lot as a car sales consultant) and running around with energy and appreciation for my mental clarity. It has been four years and I am entering my fifth and final year for my undergraduate degree in accounting. I didn’t think I could make it through the day eight years ago, now I am working on my honors thesis, training for a marathon, writing a children’s book, running a podcast, raising a puppy, living in an apartment with my boyfriend, teaching personal training, learning golf, cooking healthy meals (even for the puppy), and living with purpose. Even with all that, I still have days of sadness, unwarranted or warranted feelings that fog my mind. By talking with friends and family, about our journeys, I have decided to organize and teach how I have allowed my purpose, not my depression, to drive my life. I know my story is not your story. You have obstacles far different than mine and unfortunately, maybe not the support system I had. I am here. The entire living with purpose community is here. We are here to say that when there is a purpose, there is a way. Blog post: Purpose versus Passion I do believe purpose is different than passion. Purpose is constant while passion is variable. One can be passionate about a cause without that being your purpose. Our passion changes far more often than our purpose. For instance, my purpose is to teach self love and compassion. It is through self compassion that we can become compassionate to others. How we can expect ourselves to share an emotion towards another being that we are incapable of sharing with ourselves. To feel that you are not loved is to be lonely. We are social beings and knowing that our presence is not only accepted but embraced, is crucial for our mental health. From a young child, I have worried about others well-being and felt proud when I bring a smile to someone’s face. In contrast, my passion has changed numerous times. Passions that I was at one point certain I would pursue as a career include: dancing, horse training, teaching math, tutoring English, traditional/western medicine (Medical Doctor), and full time fitness instructor. Now, I am less than one year away from graduating with my Bachelors in Accountancy. Accountancy is not a passion of mine rather I believed getting the degree would allow me to learn about running my own business and alot me the time to learn and grow while getting my degree. While getting my degree, I have continued to follow my purpose to teach self love and compassion. Appreciating and valuing my professors, peers, and myself has allowed me to view my education with purpose not as a chore. Value the stage of your life you are in. Value the role you have. Are you a mother, employee, spouse, entrepreneur, college student, or perhaps a combination? You are surviving, but are you thriving? Let’s start thriving together. Blog Post: Discomfort and Comfort You learn about yourself by seeking new experiences. My dear friend Kristina once told me to, “Lean in to the discomfort.” When we lean in to the discomfort we are forced to search for purpose in the pain. Purpose does not come from repetition unless the action you are repeating already brings you purpose. Embark on journeys that scare you. Be okay with the unknown. Plans are good, but adaptation is necessary. Life is full of surprises. Live. Adapt. Reflect. Grow. Your purpose will follow.
